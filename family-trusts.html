Family Trusts

Some families need continuity structures that go beyond transfer and into long-range stewardship.

Family trusts may become relevant when wealth is more complex, beneficiaries have different needs, family governance matters, or the objective is not just transfer but controlled stewardship across time.

Trust Conversations Often Begin With
  • Multi-generational wealth and family governance
  • Minor children or vulnerable beneficiaries
  • Promoter-family or business-wealth complexity
  • A desire for structure beyond a simple transfer event
What A Trust Can Help Do

Trust structures are usually explored when families want more control over how wealth is held, governed, and eventually distributed.

Not every family needs a trust. But for the right families, it can become an important part of the wider continuity architecture alongside will planning, liquidity design, and family stewardship conversations.

Stewardship

Create a longer-term framework for family capital

A trust can help define how capital should be held, administered, governed, and passed on over time.

Beneficiaries

Accommodate different needs more thoughtfully

Trusts may become relevant when beneficiaries differ in age, maturity, readiness, or vulnerability.

Governance

Support family clarity where business wealth is involved

Promoter wealth or family business contexts often raise questions about control, stewardship, and decision rights across generations.

Continuity

Fit legal structuring into a broader planning design

Trusts work best when aligned with ownership patterns, liquidity, tax coordination, wills, and the family's real long-range intent.

Questions Worth Exploring
  • Does the family want governance beyond a one-time transfer?
  • Are there beneficiaries needing differentiated stewardship?
  • Is business wealth part of the continuity picture?
  • Would a trust create more order than a will alone?
Trusts In Context

A trust should not be viewed as a standalone legal object. It should fit the family's wider planning architecture.

The decision becomes stronger when it is linked to liquidity, family obligations, succession intent, portfolio structure, and long-term stewardship principles.
